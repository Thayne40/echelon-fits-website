Ethics Check 6

Bias and Fairness: Recognizing AI's Hidden Assumptions

Ensuring AI serves all members equitably in the fitness industry

My Position on AI Bias in Fitness

As someone running a gym and thinking about using AI for workout plans and member assessments, I've noticed something that really concerns me. Most AI fitness tools are trained on data from young, athletic people who are already in great shape. This means when the AI creates workout programs, it might totally miss what older adults need, or people with disabilities, or folks who are just getting back into fitness after a long break. The AI might label something as a "beginner workout" but it's really only thinking about what works for fit people, not actual beginners who might be intimidated or dealing with old injuries.

Let me give you a real example of how this could go wrong. Say I use an AI tool to build a workout plan for a new member who's 55 years old and hasn't worked out in years. The AI might recommend things like box jumps or running because that's what it learned from its training data. But it has no idea this person has bad knees or feels nervous around all the gym equipment. The AI is just doing what it thinks is "best" based on the data it saw, which was probably mostly younger people who already know their way around a gym. That's not fair to someone who genuinely needs a different starting point.

What the Bible Says About Fairness

James 2 talks about not showing favoritism to people. It says believers shouldn't treat some people better than others just because of who they are. When I think about my gym, this means everyone deserves a workout plan that actually fits their needs, not just what some algorithm thinks is right based on limited information. There's also a verse in Leviticus that says to judge people fairly and not show favoritism to anyone. When AI only knows how to help people who look like its training data, it's basically playing favorites. That goes against treating everyone with the fairness and respect they deserve.

How I'm Going to Fix This

My main solution is pretty simple but I think it'll make a huge difference. Before any AI workout plan goes to a member, one of our actual human trainers has to look it over. They need to ask themselves a few things. Does this plan make sense for what the member told us about their health and any injuries? Is this really at their fitness level or is it assuming too much? Will this plan make them feel confident or just overwhelmed and ready to quit? I also want to make sure our signup forms ask the right questions so both the AI and our trainers know about things like past injuries, health stuff, how much experience someone has, and what they're actually trying to achieve.

Another thing I want to do is change what kind of success stories we pay attention to. Instead of only celebrating the dramatic transformations that look good on Instagram, we should track all kinds of wins. Maybe it's an older member who can touch their toes again, or someone who just showed up consistently for a month, or a person with a disability who hit a goal that matters to them. If we ever train our own AI or give it data to learn from, it should see real progress from real people of all types, not just the highlight reel.

Last thing is just being honest with people. If AI helped create their workout plan, they should know that. But more importantly, they need to know they can always speak up if something doesn't feel right. They can ask for changes, talk about their concerns, or just work with a human trainer who sees them as a whole person instead of just data points. Technology should help us take better care of people, not replace the human connection and understanding that makes someone feel seen and supported.
